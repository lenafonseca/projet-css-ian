What's a gorilla?

Gorillas are great apes living in the forests and plains of Africa. They are frugivorous and can grow up to two meters in height and weigh up to 250 kilograms, making them the largest and strongest of all apes. These primates are the closest to humans from a genetic point of view.

Features

The gorilla is, after the bonobo and the chimpanzee, the closest animal to humans from a genetic point of view. This relationship has been confirmed by the similarities between chromosomes and blood groups. The human genome differs by only 1.57% from that of the gorilla. Standing upright, gorillas reach a height of 1.70 metres, but they are actually slightly taller because their knees are bent. Their arm span is longer than their body length and can reach 2.75 metres. There is a large difference in mass between the sexes: females weigh between 90 and 150 kilograms and males weigh up to 275 kilograms in mountain gorillas. Coat size depends on sex and age. Older males develop silver-grey fur on their backs, hence their name "silver backs". The coat of mountain gorillas is particularly long and silky. Like all hominoids, gorillas are tailless. Their anatomy is powerful, and their face and ears are hairless. The face has a pronounced supraorbital torus.

West African lowland gorillas

We can see them in Gabon, Guinea, Cameroon and the Central African Republic; there are between 40,000 and 80,000 of them.

East African lowland gorillas

We can see them in the Congo, and there are about 12,000 of them.

Mountain Gorillas of East Africa

We can see them in the forests of Uganda and Bwindi. They can live at more than 3000m above sea level. There are about 600 of them.
